    Donut Math: The Art of Spinning ASCII

    Ever wondered how a simple ASCII donut can spin so mesmerizingly on your screen? It's all about the math and a bit of programming magic. Let's dive into the fascinating world of torus geometry and projection to unravel this mystery.

    Mathematical Foundations

    1. Torus Geometry

    Picture a donut. Not the kind you eat, but a torus—a 3D shape defined by two radii:

    • R1: The radius of the tube
    • R2: The distance from the center of the tube to the center of the torus
    The magic begins with these parametric equations:

    x = (R2 + R1cosθ)cosφ
    y = (R2 + R1cosθ)sinφ
    z = R1sinθ

    2. 3D to 2D Projection

    To bring our 3D torus to life on a 2D screen, we project each point using:

    x' = K1 * x / (K2 + z)
    y' = K1 * y / (K2 + z)

    Here, K1 and K2 are our trusty constants, tweaking the scale and depth to perfection.

    3. Lighting and Shading

    The secret sauce? Lighting. We calculate brightness using the dot product of the surface normal and a light direction vector:

    L = cosφcosθsinB - cosAcosθsinφ - sinAsinθ + cosB(cosAsinθ - cosθsinAsinφ)

    This luminance value L is then mapped to ASCII characters: .,-~:;=!*#$@, creating the illusion of depth and texture.
